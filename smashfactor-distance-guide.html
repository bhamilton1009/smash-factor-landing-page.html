01
The most common distance leak in amateur golfers

Hip Internal Rotation Restriction

The physical finding that explains more swing faults than any other single screen

Hip internal rotation (IR) is the ability of each hip joint to rotate inward. For a right-handed golfer, this means your lead hip rotating internally through the downswing and follow-through, and your trail hip rotating internally at the top of the backswing.

When this rotation is restricted — extremely common in recreational golfers who sit for extended periods — the body compensates. Rather than rotating through the hip, the motion gets borrowed from the lower back, the knee, or the foot. The kinematic sequence breaks. Power leaks. Distance stalls. And no matter how many lessons you take, the same faults keep showing up.

🎴 Self-Assessment — Try This Now

The backswing heel test: Stand in your normal golf posture and take your backswing to the top. Pause there and look down at your trail foot.

If you checked one or more — hip IR restriction is likely a factor. The TPI screen measures exactly how much rotation you have on each side.
Swing faults this restriction causes
Early extension — body thrusts toward ball at impact
Loss of posture through the downswing
Reverse spine angle at the top
Hanging back — weight doesn't transfer

02
The hidden culprit behind pain and distance loss

Thoracic Spine Mobility Deficit

Why your coach can see the fault but your body won't cooperate

The thoracic spine — the middle portion of your back from your shoulder blades to your lower ribs — is the primary rotational engine in the golf swing. A full shoulder turn, proper hip-shoulder separation, and maintaining spine angle through impact all depend directly on T-spine mobility.

When the T-spine can't rotate freely, the body compensates by borrowing rotation from the lower back — which is not designed for rotation under load. The result is simultaneous power loss and injury risk. Back pain that shows up during or after rounds is one of the most consistent signals of a T-spine limitation driving swing compensation.

🎴 Self-Assessment — Try This Now

The seated rotation test: Sit upright in a chair with feet flat on the floor, arms crossed over your chest. Without moving your hips or lower back, rotate your upper body to the right as far as you can. Then repeat to the left.

If you checked one or more — T-spine restriction is likely present. The TPI screen measures side-to-side asymmetry that predicts both swing faults and injury risk.
Swing faults this restriction causes
Over-the-top — outside-in path from limited shoulder turn
Flat shoulder plane through the swing
Back pain during and after rounds
Chicken wing — lead arm collapses at impact

03
Your biggest power engine is probably offline

Glute Activation and Firing Pattern

Why you can have strong glutes and still not be using them in your swing

The glutes are the primary power producers in the golf swing. When your lead glute fires correctly through the downswing, it drives hip rotation, creates the deceleration that transfers energy up the kinematic chain, and stabilizes the pelvis through impact. Take the glutes offline and you're asking your arms to generate power they were never designed to produce.

Prolonged sitting causes hip flexors to tighten and reciprocally inhibit the glutes — the nervous system stops reliably activating them. You can have perfectly strong glutes and still have poor firing patterns. Strength in the gym doesn't equal activation in the swing. This shows up as a timing issue: the hips don't fully drive, you lose hip-shoulder separation, and the arms take over.

🎴 Self-Assessment — Try This Now

The single-leg glute bridge test: Lie on your back with knees bent. Lift one foot off the floor and bridge up on the other leg. Hold for 5 seconds. Feel where the work is happening.

If you checked one or more — glute activation is likely a limiting factor. The TPI screen identifies the specific firing pattern deficit and which side is most affected.

of amateur golfers in TPI research show some degree of glute inhibition — making it one of the most addressable findings with the fastest return on training investment.

Glute activation is a neuromuscular pattern, not just a strength issue. The sequence starts with activation drills — clamshells, single-leg glute bridges, and band walks — before loading with RDLs and single-leg hinges. Exercise 3 in the daily protocol below starts the activation sequence.

Why your game falls apart on the back nine

Single Leg Stability Deficit

The physical reason Kiawah's back nine is a different game than the front

The golf swing requires each leg to independently support the full weight of the body and the forces of the swing. At impact, your lead leg is bearing load, stabilizing, and posting up as the hips drive through. If the stability isn't there, the sequence compensates — and under fatigue, it collapses.

When single-leg stability is poor, you'll hold it together under low-stress conditions — the range, early in a round, when you're fresh. Under fatigue or pressure, the stability breaks and compensations cascade. It's not mental. The stabilization baseline is running out.

🎴 Self-Assessment — Try This Now

The single-leg balance test: Stand on one leg with eyes open. Hold for 10 seconds. Then try with eyes closed for 5 seconds. Repeat on the other side.

If you checked one or more — single-leg stability is likely limiting your late-round consistency. The TPI screen quantifies left-right asymmetry that predicts exactly where your swing will break down under fatigue.
Swing faults this restriction causes
Hanging back — can't post up on lead leg through impact
Slide rather than rotation through impact
Late-round and late-hole swing deterioration
Inconsistency on uneven lies and slopes

05
Speed that gets stuck before it reaches the club

Inefficient Kinematic Sequencing

Why swinging harder produces less distance — and what to do about it

Clubhead speed is not about how hard you swing. It's about how efficiently energy transfers from your body to the club. The research on elite golfers shows a consistent sequence: hips initiate, then the thorax, then the lead arm, then the club. Each segment decelerates and hands off energy to the next — like a whip.

When physical restrictions exist anywhere in the chain — restricted hips, limited T-spine, inhibited glutes, poor single-leg stability — the sequence breaks. Energy leaks. You swing harder and get less speed. Overspeed training only works when the physical foundation is in place. Loading an inefficient sequence just reinforces the inefficiency.

🎴 Self-Assessment — Try This Now

The effort-to-speed check: Think honestly about your last 10 full driver swings on the range and on the course.

Your Daily Golf Mobility Routine

One exercise per physical pattern — start today, no equipment needed

This protocol addresses all 5 physical patterns. The goal isn’t to fix everything yourself — it’s to help you feel the difference so you understand what your body is capable of. Do this daily, ideally in the morning, for best results.

These exercises address the patterns generically. The TPI screen identifies which patterns apply to your body at what severity — so you know exactly where to focus and in what order. Without that data, you’re training the right areas, but possibly in the wrong order.
01
90/90 Hip Internal Rotation Stretch
Targets: Reason 1
Sit on the floor with both legs bent at 90 degrees — front leg forward, back leg behind. Spine tall.
  1. Lean gently forward over your front shin, back flat
  2. Hold 2–3 seconds, feel the stretch in your front hip
  3. Return upright and repeat
  4. Switch sides
Feel for: The front hip opening. If you feel it in the knee, reduce how far you lean.
8
reps each side
2 sets
02
Quadruped Thoracic Rotation
Targets: Reason 2
Start on all fours, hands under shoulders, knees under hips. Place one hand behind your head.
  1. Rotate your elbow toward the opposite elbow (downward)
  2. Rotate it open toward the ceiling as far as possible
  3. Follow the movement with your eyes
  4. Return and repeat — then switch sides
Feel for: Rotation in your mid-back, not lower back. Keep hips still throughout.
10
reps each side
2 sets
03
Glute Bridge with Activation Hold
Targets: Reason 3
Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat. Hands on glutes to feel activation.
  1. Squeeze your glutes first — before you lift
  2. Drive hips toward the ceiling, maintaining the squeeze
  3. Hold at top for 3 seconds, focus on glute contraction
  4. Lower slowly and repeat
Feel for: The work in your glutes, not hamstrings or lower back.

reps · 3-sec hold
2 sets
04
Single Leg Balance Reach
Targets: Reason 4
Stand on one leg with a slight bend in the standing knee. Arms out for balance.
  1. Slowly reach your free leg forward, then to the side, then behind
  2. Return to center after each reach without touching down
  3. Keep your standing foot, ankle, and knee stable throughout
  4. Switch sides
Feel for: Small stabilizing muscles around your ankle and hip engaging.

05
Hip-Lead Rotation Drill
Targets: Reason 5
Stand in golf posture, arms crossed over chest. Focus on your sequencing pattern.
  1. Start the rotation by deliberately firing your lead hip first
  2. Let the rotation move up — hips, then torso, then shoulders
  3. Feel the separation between hips and shoulders at initiation
  4. Pause at impact position and check hip vs shoulder position
Feel for: Hips leading. If it feels unnatural, your sequencing has been arms-dominant.

Your Pre-Round Warm-Up

Course-ready in 8 minutes — do this before every round

This sequence activates the specific physical patterns that matter most for your first tee shot and carry through all 18 holes. No equipment. 8 minutes. Do it anywhere on the course.

Save this page to your phone or print it. Golfers who use this consistently report better first-hole performance and — more importantly — better back-nine consistency.
01
Hip Circle Activation
Hip IR · Glute Activation
Stand feet shoulder-width apart, hands on hips. Make slow deliberate circles — 5 forward, 5 backward. Feel both hips opening and loading. Increase range gradually.
10 circles each direction · 60 seconds
02
Standing T-Spine Rotation
Thoracic Mobility
Stand tall, arms crossed over chest. Rotate upper body left and right, gradually increasing range. Keep hips pointing forward — isolate the rotation to your mid-back.
10 rotations each side · 60 seconds
03
Single-Leg Glute Activation
Glute Firing · Single Leg Stability
Stand on one leg, slight knee bend. Squeeze your standing glute hard and hold for 3 seconds. This primes the exact firing pattern needed at impact.
8 holds each side · 3 seconds each · 90 seconds
04
Golf Posture Hip Hinge
Hip Mobility · Sequencing
Take your golf address posture. Without a club, hinge from your hips back and forward slowly, maintaining spine angle. Rehearses the posture maintenance pattern.
10 slow reps · 60 seconds
05
Slow-Motion Hip-Lead Swings
Kinematic Sequencing
Take 5 practice swings at 20% speed, consciously initiating with your lead hip. Feel hips clearing first, then torso, then arms. Programming the correct sequence before your first full swing.
5 slow swings · 60 seconds
06
Progressive Speed Build
Full Activation
Take 6 swings building from 40% to 80% to 95% speed. Each swing carries the sequencing pattern. Your last two swings should feel effortless — that's the signal your body is primed.
6 swings · building speed · 90 seconds
